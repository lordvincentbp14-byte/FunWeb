Energy

“Energy is the power to cause change — the invisible thread that weaves motion, force, and matter together within the FunWeb.”

Definition

Energy is the ability to do work or cause change. It exists in many forms — mechanical, potential, kinetic, thermal, electrical, and chemical — representing the capacity to make something happen.

Work and energy are related: W = F × d, where W is work, F is force, and d is displacement. The SI unit of energy is joule (J), where 1 J = 1 N·m.

Sub-Concepts of Energy

Sub-Concept Description Connection to Other Nodes
Kinetic Energy (KE) Energy due to motion, increases with mass and speed. KE = 1/2 m v² Connects Mass ↔ Motion ↔ Acceleration
Potential Energy (PE) Stored energy due to position. PE = m g h Links Mass ↔ Gravity ↔ Height
Mechanical Energy Sum of kinetic and potential energy in a system. Connects Force ↔ Work ↔ Motion
Thermal Energy Energy from particle movement; linked to temperature. Links Energy ↔ Matter ↔ Heat
Electrical Energy Movement of charges through a conductor. Connects Force ↔ Motion ↔ Electricity
Law of Conservation of Energy Energy cannot be created or destroyed — only transformed. Links all nodes; Energy Web Backbone

Mathematical Relationships

  • Kinetic Energy: KE = 1/2 m v² (depends on mass and velocity; doubles in speed → quadruples KE)
  • Potential Energy: PE = m g h (depends on mass, gravitational acceleration, and height)
  • Mechanical Energy Conservation: ME = KE + PE = constant
  • Work-Energy Theorem: W = ΔKE

Real-Life Applications of Energy

  • Transportation: Fuel energy → mechanical energy in engines
  • Sports: Chemical energy in food → kinetic energy in motion
  • Roller Coasters: PE at top → KE as it descends
  • Electric Circuits: Electrical energy powers motion and light
  • Renewable Sources: Solar, wind, and hydro convert natural energy to usable power

Common Misconceptions About Energy

Misconception FunWeb Clarification
Energy is only about movement. Energy also exists in stored and potential forms.
Energy disappears when an object stops. Energy transforms into other forms, e.g., heat or sound.
Heavier objects always have more energy. Depends on both mass and velocity (KE) or height (PE).
Work and energy are unrelated. Work transfers energy between systems.
